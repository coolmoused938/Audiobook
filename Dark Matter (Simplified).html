Chapter 1

I love Thursday nights.

They have a feel to them that’s outside of time.

It’s our tradition, just the three of us—family night.

My son, Charlie, is sitting at the table, drawing on a sketch pad. He’s almost fifteen. The kid grew two inches over the summer, and he’s as tall as I am now.

I turn away from the onion I’m slicing, ask, “Can I see?”

He holds up the pad, shows me a mountain range that looks like something on another planet.

I say, “Love that. Just for fun?”

“Class project. Due tomorrow.”

“Then get back to it, Mr. Last Minute.”

Standing happy and slightly drunk in my kitchen, I’m unaware that tonight is the end of all of this. The end of everything I know, everything I love.

No one tells you it’s all about to change, to be taken away. There’s no warning, no sign that you’re standing on the edge. And maybe that’s what makes tragedy so sad. Not just what happens, but how it happens: a surprise hit that comes at you out of nowhere, when you’re least expecting it. No time to move or prepare.

The track lights shine on the surface of my wine, and the onion is beginning to sting my eyes. Thelonious Monk plays on the old record player in the den. There’s a richness to the old recording I can never get enough of, especially the crackle of noise between tracks. The den is filled with stacks and stacks of rare records that I keep telling myself I’ll get around to organizing one of these days.

My wife, Daniela, sits on the kitchen island, swirling her almost-empty wineglass in one hand and holding her phone in the other. She feels my stare and grins without looking up from the screen.

“I know,” she says. “I’m breaking the main rule of family night.”

“What’s so important?” I ask.

She puts her dark, Spanish eyes on mine. “Nothing.”

I walk over to her, take the phone softly out of her hand, and set it on the countertop.

“You could start the pasta,” I say.

“I prefer to watch you cook.”

“Yeah?” Quieter: “Turns you on, huh?”

“No, it’s just more fun to drink and do nothing.”

Her breath is wine-sweet, and she has one of those smiles that seem like they shouldn't exist. It still amazes me.

I finish my glass. “We should open more wine, right?”

“It would be silly not to.”

As I pull the cork from a new bottle, she picks her phone back up and shows me the screen. “I was reading Chicago Magazine ’s review of Marsha Altman’s show.”

“Were they kind?”

“Yeah, it’s basically a love letter.”

“Good for her.”

“I always thought…” She lets the sentence die, but I know where it was headed. Fifteen years ago, before we met, Daniela was a comer to Chicago’s art scene. She had a studio in Bucktown, showed her work in a half-dozen galleries, and had just lined up her first solo show in New York. Then came life. Me. Charlie. A bout of very bad depression after giving birth.

Derailment.

Now she teaches private art lessons to middle-grade students.

“It’s not that I’m not happy for her. I mean, she’s very smart, she deserves it all.”

I say, “If it makes you feel any better, Ryan Holder just won the Pavia Prize.”

“What’s that?”

“An award given for achievements in many areas of science. Ryan won for his work in the study of the brain.”

“Is it a big deal?”

“Million dollars. Praise. Opens the floodgates to grant money.”

“Hotter TAs?”

“Clearly that’s the real prize. He asked me to a small casual party tonight, but I refused.”

“Why?”

“Because it’s our night.”

“You should go.”

“I’d really rather not.”

Daniela lifts her empty glass. “So what you’re saying is, we both have good reason to drink a lot of wine tonight.”

I kiss her, and then pour a lot from the newly opened bottle.

“You could’ve won that prize,” Daniela says.

“You could’ve owned this city’s art scene.”

“But we did this.” She points at the high-ceilinged space of our brownstone. I bought it before Daniela with money left to me. “And we did that,” she says, pointing to Charlie as he draws with a beautiful focus that reminds me of Daniela when she’s deep in a painting.

It’s a strange thing, being the parent of a teenager. One thing to raise a little boy, another completely when a person about to be an adult looks to you for knowledge. I feel like I have little to give. I know there are fathers who see the world a certain way, with clearness and trust, who know just what to say to their sons and daughters. But I’m not one of them. The older I get, the less I understand. I love my son. He means everything to me. And yet, I can’t escape the feeling that I’m letting him down. Sending him off into the world with nothing but the small bits of my unsure view.

I move to the cabinet beside the sink, open it, and start looking for a box of fettuccine.

Daniela turns to Charlie, says, “Your father could have won the Nobel.”

I laugh. “That’s possibly an overstatement.”

“Charlie, don’t be tricked. He’s a genius.”

“You’re sweet,” I say. “And a little tipsy.”

“It’s true, and you know it. Science is less advanced because you love your family.”

I can only smile. When Daniela drinks, three things happen: her native accent begins to show, she becomes aggressively kind, and she tends toward exaggeration.

“Your father said to me one night—never forget it—that pure research is life-taking. He said…” For a moment, and to my surprise, feeling overtakes her. Her eyes get watery, and she shakes her head like she always does when she’s about to cry. At the last second, she recovers, pushes through. “He said, ‘Daniela, on my deathbed I would rather have memories of you than of a cold, empty lab.’ ”

I look at Charlie, catch him rolling his eyes as he sketches.

Probably embarrassed by our show of parental drama.

I stare into the cabinet and wait for the pain in my throat to go away.

When it does, I grab the pasta and close the door.

Daniela drinks her wine.

Charlie draws.

She just laughs, says, “Dinner will be on the table by the time you get home.”

“Which means I should be back here in…”

“Forty-five minutes.”

“What would I be without you?”

She kisses me.

“Let’s not even think about it.”

I grab my keys and wallet from the ceramic dish beside the microwave and move into the dining room, my gaze landing on the tesseract chandelier above the dinner table. Daniela gave it to me for our tenth wedding anniversary. Best gift ever.

As I reach the front door, Daniela shouts, “Return bringing ice cream!”

“Mint chocolate chip!” Charlie says.

I lift my arm, raise my thumb.

I don’t look back.

I don’t say goodbye.

And this moment slips past unnoticed.

The end of everything I know, everything I love.

—

I’ve lived in Logan Square for twenty years, and it doesn’t get any better than the first week of October. It always puts me in mind of that F. Scott Fitzgerald line: Life starts all over again when it gets crisp in the fall.

The evening is cool, and the skies are clear enough to see a handful of stars. The bars are more lively than usual, packed with sad Cubs fans.

I stop on the sidewalk in the glow of a flashy sign that blinks VILLAGE TAP and stare through the open doorway of the common corner bar you’ll find in any good Chicago neighborhood. This one happens to be my local hangout. It’s the closest to home—a few blocks from my brownstone.

I pass through the glow of the blue neon sign in the front window and step through the doorway.

Matt, the bartender and owner, nods to me as I move down the bar, making my way through the crowd that surrounds Ryan Holder.

I say to Ryan, “I was just telling Daniela about you.”

He smiles, looking very well-kept for the lecture circuit—fit and tan in a black turtleneck, his facial hair carefully shaped.

“Wow is it good to see you. I’m touched that you came. Darling?” He touches the bare shoulder of the young woman sitting on the stool beside his. “Would you mind letting my dear old friend take your chair for a minute?”

The woman readily gives up her seat, and I climb onto the stool beside Ryan.

He calls the bartender over. “We want you to give us a pair of the most costly drinks in the house.”

“Ryan, not necessary.”

He grabs my arm. “We’re drinking the best tonight.”

Matt says, “I have Macallan Twenty-Five.”

“Doubles. My bill.”

When the bartender goes, Ryan hits me in the arm. Hard. You wouldn’t think of him as a scientist at first glance. He played lacrosse during his college years, and he still has the wide-shouldered body and easy movement of a natural athlete.

“How’s Charlie and the lovely Daniela?”

“They’re great.”

“You should’ve brought her down. I haven’t seen her since last Christmas.”

“She sends along her congrats.”

“You got a good woman there, but that’s not exactly news.”

“What are the chances of you settling down in the near future?”

“Small. The single life, and its many benefits, seems to fit me. You’re still at Lakemont College?”

“Yeah.”

“Decent school. Undergrad physics, right?”

“Exactly.”

“So you’re teaching…”

“Quantum mechanics. Basic stuff mainly. Nothing too terribly exciting.”

Matt returns with our drinks, and Ryan takes them out of his hands and sets mine before me.

“So this celebration…,” I say.

“Just a sudden thing a few of my postgrads threw together. They love nothing more than to get me drunk and talking.”

“Big year for you, Ryan. I still remember you almost failing differential equations.”

“And you helped me. More than once.”

For a second, behind the confidence and the smoothness, I glimpse the silly, fun-loving grad student with whom I shared a messy apartment for a year and a half.

I ask, “Was the Pavia Prize for your work in—”

“Finding the prefrontal cortex as a consciousness maker.”

“Right. Of course. I read your paper on it.”

“What’d you think?”

“Amazing.”

He looks genuinely pleased at the compliment.

“If I’m honest, Jason, and there’s no fake humbleness here, I always thought it would be you publishing the most important papers.”

“Really?”

He studies me over the top of his black plastic glass frames.

“Of course. You’re smarter than I am. Everyone knew it.”

I drink my whisky. I try not to acknowledge how delicious it is.

He says, “Just a question, but do you see yourself more as a research scientist or a teacher these days?”

“I—”

“Because I see myself, first and foremost, as a man searching for answers to basic questions. Now, if the people around me”—he gestures at his students who have begun to crowd in—“are smart enough to take in knowledge by just being near me…great. But the giving of knowledge, as it were, doesn’t interest me. All that matters is the science. The research.”

I note a flicker of annoyance, or anger, in his voice, and it’s building, like he’s getting himself worked up toward something.

I try to laugh it off. “Are you upset with me, Ryan? It almost sounds like you think I let you down.”

“Look, I’ve taught at MIT, Harvard, Johns Hopkins, the best schools on the planet. I’ve met the smartest people in the room, and Jason, you would’ve changed the world if you’d decided to go that path. If you’d stuck with it. Instead, you’re teaching undergrad physics to future doctors and patent lawyers.”

“We can’t all be superstars like you, Ryan.”

“Not if you quit.”

I finish my whisky.

“Well, I’m so glad I popped in for this.” I step down off the barstool.

“Don’t be that way, Jason. I was paying you a compliment.”

“I’m proud of you, man. I mean that.”

“Jason.”

“Thanks for the drink.”

Back outside, I walk down the sidewalk. The more distance I put between myself and Ryan, the angrier I become.

And I’m not even sure at whom.

My face is hot.

Lines of sweat trail down my sides.

Without thinking, I step into the street against a crosswalk signal and instantly hear the sound of tires locking up, of rubber screeching across pavement.

I turn and stare in disbelief as a yellow cab rushes toward me.

Through the approaching windshield, I see the cabbie so clearly—a mustached man, wide-eyed with obvious panic, bracing for impact.

And then my hands are flat against the warm, yellow metal of the hood and the cabbie is leaning out his window, yelling at me, “You dipshit, you almost died! Pull your head out of your ass!”

Horns begin to blare behind the cab.

I go back to the sidewalk and watch the flow of traffic resume.

The occupants of three separate cars are kind enough to slow down so they can flip me off.

—

Whole Foods smells like the hippie I dated before Daniela—a mix of fresh produce, ground coffee, and essential oils.

The scare with the cab has ruined my mood, and I look at the freezer cases in something of a fog, tired and sleepy.

It feels colder when I’m back outside, a strong wind blowing in off the lake, suggesting the bad winter that is coming very soon.

With my canvas bag filled with ice cream, I take a different route toward home. It adds six blocks, but what I lose in shortness, I gain in being alone, and between the cab and Ryan, I need some extra time to calm down.

I pass a building site, not being worked on for the night, and a few blocks later, the playground of the grade school my son went to, the metal sliding board shining under a streetlamp and the swings moving in the breeze.

There’s a feeling to these fall nights that touches something basic inside of me. Something from a long time ago. From my childhood in western Iowa. I think of high school football games and the stadium lights shining down on the players. I smell ripening apples, and the sour smell of beer from keg parties in the cornfields. I feel the wind in my face as I ride in the back of an old pickup truck down a country road at night, dust swirling red in the taillights and the entire span of my life opening out ahead of me.

It’s the great thing about being young.

There’s a feeling of no weight that fills everything because no bad choices have been made, no paths decided on, and the road forking out ahead is pure, unlimited possibility.

I love my life, but I haven’t felt that lightness in a long time. Fall nights like this are as close as I get.

The cold is beginning to clear my head.

It will be good to be home again. I’m thinking of starting up the gas logs. We’ve never had a fire before Halloween, but tonight is so unusually cold that after walking a mile in this wind, all I want is to sit by the fire with Daniela and Charlie and a glass of wine.

The street goes under the El.

I pass under the rusting metal of the railway.

For me, even more than the skyline, the El is a symbol of the city.

This is my favorite section of the walk home, because it’s the darkest and quietest.

At the moment…

No trains coming in.

No headlights in either direction.

No sound of a pub.

Nothing but the far-off roar of a jet overhead, coming in to land at O’Hare.

Wait…

There’s something coming—steps on the sidewalk.

I look back.

A shadow runs toward me, the distance between us getting smaller faster than I can understand what’s happening.

The first thing I see is a face.

Pale white.

High, curved eyebrows that look drawn.

Red, tight lips—too thin, too perfect.

And scary eyes—big and pitch-black, without pupils or irises.

The second thing I see is the barrel of a gun, four inches from the end of my nose.

The low, rough voice behind the geisha mask says, “Turn around.”

I pause, too shocked to move.

He pushes the gun into my face.

I turn around.

Before I can tell him that my wallet is in my front left pocket, he says, “I’m not here for your money. Start walking.”

I start walking.

“Faster.”

I walk faster.

“What do you want?” I ask.

“Keep your mouth shut.”

A train roars past overhead, and we emerge from the darkness under the El, my heart jumping inside my chest. I take in my surroundings with a sudden and deep interest. Across the street is a gated townhome complex, and this side of the block has a group of businesses that close at five.

A nail salon.

A law office.

An appliance fix shop.

A tire store.

This neighborhood is a ghost town, nobody out.

“See that SUV?” he asks. There’s a black Lincoln Navigator parked on the curb just ahead. The alarm beeps. “Get in the driver’s seat.”

“Whatever you’re thinking about doing—”

“Or you can bleed to death right here on the sidewalk.”

I open the driver’s-side door and slide in behind the wheel.

“My grocery bag,” I say.

“Bring it.” He climbs in behind me. “Start the car.”

I pull the door closed and put the canvas Whole Foods bag in the front passenger floorboard. It’s so quiet in the car I can actually hear my pulse—a fast beating against my eardrum.

“What are you waiting for?” he asks.

I press the engine-start button.

“Turn on the navigation.”

I turn it on.

“Click on ‘past places.’ ”

I’ve never owned a car with built-in GPS, and it takes me a moment to find the right tab on the screen.

Three locations appear.

One is my home address. One is the university where I work.

“You’ve been following me?” I ask.

“Choose Pulaski Drive.”

I select 1400 Pulaski Drive, Chicago, Illinois 60616, with no idea where that even is. The female voice on the GPS tells me: Make a legal U-turn when possible and go for point-eight miles.

Shifting into gear, I turn out into the dark street.

The man behind me says, “Buckle your seat belt.”

I strap myself in as he does the same.

“Jason, just so we’re clear, if you do anything other than follow these directions exactly, I’m going to shoot you through the seat. Do you understand what I’m telling you?”

“Yes.”

I drive us through my neighborhood, wondering if I’m seeing it all for the last time.

At a red light, I pull to a stop in front of my corner bar. Through the deeply tinted front passenger window, I see the door is still propped open. I glimpse Matt, and through the crowd, Ryan, turned around in his stool now, his back to the bar, his elbows on the scuffed wood, holding court for his postgrads. Probably thrilling them with a scary story of failure starring his old roommate.

I want to call out to him. To make him understand that I’m in trouble. That I need—

“Green light, Jason.”

I accelerate through the intersection.

The GPS navigation guides us east through Logan Square to the Kennedy Expressway, where the indifferent female voice tells me, Turn right in one hundred feet and go for nineteen-point-eight miles.

Southbound traffic is light enough for me to set the speedometer at seventy and keep it there. My hands sweat on the leather steering wheel, and I can’t stop wondering, Am I going to die tonight?

It occurs to me that if I do survive, I’ll carry a new understanding with me for the rest of my days: we leave this life the same way we enter it—totally alone, without help. I’m afraid, and there is nothing Daniela or Charlie or anyone can do to help me in this moment when I need them more than ever. They don’t even know what I’m experiencing.

The interstate goes around the western edge of downtown. The Willis Tower and its group of smaller skyscrapers shine with a calm warmth against the night.

Through the twisting panic and fear, my mind races, fighting to figure out what’s happening.

My address is in the GPS. So this wasn’t a chance meeting. This man has been following me. Knows me. So, some action of mine has resulted in this outcome.

But which?

I’m not rich.

My life isn’t worth anything beyond its value to me and to my loved ones.

I’ve never been arrested, never committed a crime.

Never slept with another man’s wife.

Sure, I flip people off in traffic sometimes, but that’s just Chicago.

My last and only physical fight was in the sixth grade when I punched a classmate in the nose for pouring milk down the back of my shirt.

I haven’t hurt anyone in the important sense of the word. In a way that might have ended with me driving a Lincoln Navigator with a gun pointed at the back of my head.

I’m an atomic physicist and professor at a small college.

I don’t treat my students, even the worst of the bunch, with anything but respect. Those who have failed out of my classes failed because they didn’t care in the first place, and certainly none of them could accuse me of ruining their lives. I go out of my way to help my students pass.

The skyline gets smaller in the side mirror, falling farther and farther away like a familiar and comforting piece of coastline.

I say, “Did I do something to you in the past? Or someone you work for? I just don’t understand what you could possibly want from—”

“The more you talk, the worse it will be for you.”

For the first time, I realize there’s something familiar in his voice. I can’t for the life of me figure out when or where, but we’ve met. I’m sure of it.

I feel the shaking of my phone getting a text message.

Then another.

And another.

He forgot to take my phone.

I look at the time: 9:05 p.m.

I left my house a little over an hour ago. It’s Daniela no doubt, wondering where I am. I’m fifteen minutes late, and I’m never late.

I look in the rearview mirror, but it’s too dark to see anything except a sliver of the ghost-white mask. I try something. Taking my left hand off the steering wheel, I place it in my lap and count to ten.

He says nothing.

I put my hand back on the wheel.

That computerized voice breaks the silence: Merge right onto the Eighty-Seventh Street exit in four-point-three miles.

Again, I take my left hand slowly off the wheel.

This time, I slide it into the pocket of my tan pants. My phone is buried deep, and I just barely touch it with my first and second fingers, somehow managing to pinch it between them.

Little by little, I pull it out, the rubber cover catching on every fold of cloth, and now a constant shaking rattling between my fingertips—a call coming in.

When I finally work it free, I place my phone faceup in my lap and return my hand to the steering wheel.

As the navigation voice updates the distance from our upcoming turn, I shoot a glance down at the phone.

There’s a missed call from “Dani” and three texts:

DANI 2m ago

Dinner’s on the table

DANI 2m ago

Hurry home we are STARVING!

DANI 1m ago

You get lost? :)

I focus my attention on the road, wondering if the light from my phone is visible from the backseat.

The screen goes dark.

Reaching down, I press the ON/OFF button and swipe the screen. I type in my four-digit code, press the green “Messages” icon. Daniela’s chat is at the top, and as I open our talk, my kidnapper moves behind me.

I grab the wheel with both hands again.

Merge right onto the Eighty-Seventh Street exit in one-point-nine miles.

The screensaver times out, auto-lock starts, my phone goes black.

Shit.

Sliding my hand back down, I retype the code and begin typing out the most important text of my life, my finger clumsy on the screen, each word taking two or three tries to complete as auto-correct messes things up.

The barrel of the gun pushes into the back of my head.

I react, turning into the fast lane.

“What are you doing, Jason?”

I straighten the wheel with one hand, moving us back into the slow lane as my other hand lowers toward the phone, getting close to Send.

He jumps between the front seats, his gloved hand reaching around my waist, taking the phone away.

Merge right onto the Eighty-Seventh Street exit in five hundred feet.

“What’s your passcode, Jason?” When I don’t respond, he says, “Wait. I bet I know this. Month and year of your birthday backwards? Let’s see…three-seven-two-one. There we go.”

In the rearview mirror, I see the phone light up his mask.

He reads the text he stopped me from sending: “ ‘1400 Pulaski call 91…’ Bad boy.”

I turn onto the interstate off-ramp.

The GPS says, Turn left onto Eighty-Seventh Street and go east for three-point-eight miles.

We drive into South Chicago, through a neighborhood we have no reason to be in.

Past rows of factory homes.

Apartment buildings.

Empty parks with rusted swing sets and basketball hoops without nets.

Shops locked up for the night behind security gates.

Gang writing everywhere.

He asks, “So do you call her Dani or Daniela?”

My throat tightens.

Anger and worry and weakness growing inside of me.

“Jason, I asked you a question.”

“Go to hell.”

He leans close, his words hot in my ear. “You do not want to go down this road with me. I will hurt you worse than you’ve ever been hurt in your life. Pain you didn’t even know was possible. What do you call her?”

I clench my teeth. “Daniela.”

“Never Dani? Even though that’s what’s on your phone?”

I’m tempted to flip the car at high speed and just kill us both.

I say, “Rarely. She doesn’t like it.”

“What’s in the grocery bag?”

“Why do you want to know what I call her?”

“What’s in the bag?”

“Ice cream.”

“It’s family night, right?”

“Yeah.”

In the rearview mirror, I see him typing on my phone.

“What are you writing?” I ask.

He doesn’t respond.

We’re out of the poor area now, riding through a empty place that doesn’t even feel like Chicago anymore, with the skyline nothing but a smear of light on the far horizon. The houses are falling apart, dark, and lifeless. Everything long left behind.

We cross a river and straight ahead lies Lake Michigan, its black space a fitting end of this city emptiness.

As if the world ends right here.

And perhaps mine does.

Turn right and go south on Pulaski Drive for half a mile to destination.

He laughs to himself. “Wow, are you in trouble with the wife.” I squeeze the steering wheel. “Who was that man you had whisky with tonight, Jason? I couldn’t tell from outside.”

It’s so dark out here in this borderland between Chicago and Indiana.

We’re passing the remains of railroad yards and factories.

“Jason.”

“His name is Ryan Holder. He used to be—”

“Your old roommate.”

“How’d you know that?”

“Are you two close? I don’t see him in your contacts.”

“Not really. How do you—?”

“I know almost everything about you, Jason. You could say I’ve made your life my focus.”

“Who are you?”

You will arrive at your destination in five hundred feet.

“Who are you?”

He doesn’t answer, but my attention is beginning to pull away from him as I focus on our increasingly far away surroundings.

The pavement flows under the SUV’s headlights.

Empty behind us.

Empty ahead.

There’s the lake off to my left, deserted warehouses on my right.

You have arrived at your destination.

I stop the Navigator in the middle of the road.

He says, “The entrance is up ahead on the left.”

The headlights touch a shaky stretch of twelve-foot fencing, topped with a crown of rusted barbed wire. The gate is ajar, and a chain that once locked it shut has been cut and coiled in the weeds by the roadside.

“Just push the gate with the front bumper.”

Even from inside the nearly silent interior of the SUV, the screech of the gate grinding open is loud. The cones of light show the remains of a road, the pavement cracked and bent from years of harsh Chicago winters.

I engage the high beams.

Light washes over a parking lot, where streetlamps have fallen everywhere like spilled matchsticks.

Beyond, a large building looms.

The brick front of the old building is beside huge round tanks and a pair of hundred-foot smokestacks going into the sky.

“What is this place?” I ask.

“Put it in PARK and turn it off.”

I bring the car to a stop, shift out of gear, and punch off the engine.

It becomes very silent.

“What is this place?” I ask again.

“What are your Friday plans?”

“Excuse me?”

A sharp hit to the side of my head sends me falling into the steering wheel, shocked and wondering for a half second if this is what it feels like to be shot in the head.

But no, he only hit me with his gun.

I touch my hand to the point of the hit.

I realize that I see the interior of the Navigator as a kind of lifeboat. As long as I stay inside, he can’t really hurt me.

He won’t make a mess in here.

“Jason.”

My chest is heaving, I’m starting to breathe fast, black spots exploding across my view.

“I know what you’re thinking,” he says, “and I can hurt you just as easily inside this car.”

I’m not getting enough air. I’m starting to panic.

But I manage to say, breathlessly, “Nonsense. You don’t want my blood in here.”

—

When I wake up, he’s pulling me out of the front seat by my arms. He drops me in the gravel, where I sit confused, waiting for my head to clear.

It’s always colder near the lake, and tonight is no exception. The wind gives a sharp, jagged bite on my exposed skin, which is covered in gooseflesh.

It’s so dark out here I can see five times the number of stars as in the city.

My head is pounding, and a fresh line of blood runs down the side of my face. But with a full load of adrenaline rushing through my system, the pain is quiet.

He drops a flashlight in the dirt beside me and shines his at the falling-apart building I saw as we drove in. “After you.”

I clutch the light in my hand and struggle to my feet. Stumbling toward the building, my bare feet trample wet newspaper. I dodge crumpled beer cans and lines of glass that shine under the beam.

Approaching the main entrance, I imagine this empty parking lot on another night. A night to come. It’s early winter, and through a curtain of falling snow, the darkness is striped with flashing blues and reds. Detectives and cadaver dogs swarm the ruins, and as they examine my body somewhere inside, naked and decayed and cut up, a patrol car parks in front of my brownstone in Logan Square. It’s two in the morning, and Daniela comes to the door in a nightgown. “I don’t know.” Probably best not to mention the lab I think I just got away from.

“No.”

“I don’t know where it is.”

He grabs the clipboard. “Give me your wife’s name again. And phone number. We’ll try to call her for you.”

I spell Daniela’s name and say her cell number and our home number as my blood rushes into a plastic vial.

“You’re going to scan my head?” I ask. “See what’s going on?”

“Absolutely.”

They give me a private room on the eighth floor.

I clean up my face in the bathroom, take off my shoes, and climb into bed.

Sleep pulls, but the thinker in my brain won’t shut down.

I can’t stop thinking.

Making ideas and breaking them down.

Trying to make sense of everything that’s happened.

In this moment, I have no way of knowing what’s real and what isn’t. I can’t even be sure that I was ever married.

No. Wait.

I raise my left hand and study my ring finger.

The ring is gone, but the proof of its existence stays as a faint dent around the base of my finger. It was there. It left a mark. That means someone took it.

I touch the dent, knowing both the fear and the ease of what it shows—the last bit of my reality.

I wonder—

What will happen when this last real trace of my marriage is gone?

When there’s no hold?

As the skies above Chicago move toward dawn—a sad, cloud-covered purple—I lose myself to sleep.
